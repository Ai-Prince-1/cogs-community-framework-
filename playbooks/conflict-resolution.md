# Handling Conflict in COGS Circles

**Clear, human-scale processes for addressing tension before it becomes toxic.**

Conflict is normal in any real human group. The COGS Framework treats it as a signal to improve, not a failure. The goal is to resolve issues at the smallest scale possible, with honesty and goodwill.

---

## Core Principles

- **Name it early** — Unspoken tension becomes the invisible culture of the group.
- **Smallest scale first** — Direct → Circle → Mesh.
- **Goodwill required** — Assume positive intent unless proven otherwise.
- **Valid Objection standard** — Only specific, demonstrable harm blocks a decision (not preferences or discomfort).
- **Easy exit** — Leaving is always clean and without shame.

---

## The Three-Level Conflict Protocol

| Level | When to Use | How to Handle |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **1. Direct Conversation** | First instance of tension between two people | The two people speak privately, face-to-face or via call (not text). Use the Difficult Conversations guidelines below. |
| **2. Full Circle Mediation** | Unresolved after 1–2 attempts | Bring it to the full Circle. The current Facilitator structures a dedicated conversation using consent-based decision making. |
| **3. Mesh Mediation** | Still unresolved after Circle mediation | The Bridge requests a neutral third-party Circle from the Mesh to assist. |

If Mesh mediation also fails, the **Seeding Ceremony** (splitting into two new Circles) is a dignified and supported option.

---

## Consent-Based Decision Making

Used when a decision matters and disagreement appears:

- A proposal moves forward when there are **no Valid Objections**.
- **Valid Objection** = specific, demonstrable harm to the Circle or its members.
- Preferences, discomfort, or “I just don’t like it” are **not** valid objections — they are perspectives to discuss.

If Valid Objections exist, the group modifies the proposal to address them. If it can’t be resolved, set it aside and revisit at the Season Retrospective.

---

## Difficult Conversations — Practical Guidance

Use these patterns to keep conversations productive:

1. Start with observation, not judgment: “I noticed…” instead of “You always…”
2. Separate the person from the pattern.
3. Name the impact before proposing a solution.
4. Ask before advising: “Would it be helpful if I shared what I think, or do you just need to be heard?”
5. Close the loop — confirm both people feel heard.

**Facilitator tip**: When tension arises in a gathering, you are empowered to name it gently and create space for it.

---

## When a Member Needs to Leave

- Leaving is always allowed with **no shame, no social debt, and no explanation required**.
- A simple notification to the group is sufficient.
- The Circle acknowledges the contribution of the departing member with respect.
- If someone disappears without communication for 3+ weeks, the Facilitator reaches out once, warmly, with no pressure.

---

## Red Flags (Anti-Cult Protocols)

Name these immediately if observed (see full list in Handbook Section 8):

- Pressure to adopt specific beliefs
- Treating any member as a permanent authority
- Discouraging outside relationships or perspectives
- Financial pressure or personal enrichment
- Suppression of dissent
- Shame around leaving or questioning

Any member can raise a red flag at any time. The Facilitator must pause the agenda and make space to address it.

---

## Prevention > Cure

The best conflict resolution is prevention:

- Strong weekly **The Open** and **The Share** build trust and surface issues early.
- Rotating Facilitator role prevents power concentration.
- Season Retrospectives regularly ask: “Are we becoming more open or more closed?”
- Clear roles and lightweight rituals create psychological safety.
