# COGS Framework Glossary

## Core Concepts

### Circle
A group of 3–7 individual adults who meet regularly, practice together, and take genuine responsibility for each other's growth and wellbeing. Each member holds an equal footprint. The core unit of the COGS Framework. No one's voice is more important than another's.

### Mesh
Up to 7 Circles (~49 people maximum) that choose to coordinate. The community-layer coordination group. A Mesh shares learning across Circles, coordinates larger seasonal projects, supports mediation when needed, and maintains a distributed registry of active Circles. The Mesh has no permanent leadership and no authority over individual Circle decisions.

### Assembly
Up to 3 Meshes (~147 people maximum) that form a tribal-layer gathering. The Assembly convenes once a year or when a challenge arises that no single Mesh can resolve alone. It is a gathering place for celebration, cross-Mesh learning, and collective reflection. The Assembly holds no authority over any Mesh or Circle.

### Season
A 12-week focused cycle of shared learning and practice. Each Season has a clear beginning (Season Opening), middle (mid-Season check-in), and end (Season Retrospective). Circles choose 2–3 life domains to focus on each Season.

### Level 1
The zero-friction core of the COGS Framework. Requires only a small group of people, a shared commitment to showing up, and 70 minutes a week. Most Circles run on Level 1 indefinitely. No software. No budget. No formal hierarchy.

### Level 2
Event-driven governance modules that activate only when specific real-world conditions are met. They are protections, not requirements. The three modules are: Scale Trigger (8th member), Capital Trigger (shared resources), and Friction Trigger (unresolved conflict).

## Weekly Structure

### The Open
A 10-minute check-in round at the start of each gathering. One sentence each: *How are you arriving today?* No advice. No responses. No cross-talk. Just listening. This creates the safety and presence needed for the rest of the gathering.

### The Share
A 45-minute segment where each member reports on the past week in the active seasonal domains. What was tried? What worked? What failed? What was learned? Members report honestly on their real-world experience, not advice or interpretation.

### The Commit
A 15-minute segment where each member names one specific, self-chosen action for the coming week. Concrete. Measurable. Achievable by the next gathering. Vague intentions are pushed back on gently: *"Can you make that more specific?"*

### The Hand-Off
The moment at the end of the gathering when the current Facilitator explicitly names next week's Facilitator. This is how the rotation stays alive and intentional.

### The Accountability Ledger
A living record of commitments made each week. Immediately after each gathering (within one hour if possible), each member posts their weekly commitment to the group's shared thread using a simple format: Domain + specific action. The group's message history becomes the ledger. No Scribe required at Level 1.

## Seasonal Structure

### Season Opening
The first gathering of a 12-week Season (90–120 minutes). Sets the tone, domains, and commitments for the next 12 weeks. Includes check-in round, domain selection, personal intentions, role assignments, resource conversation, and outward support conversation.

### Mid-Season Check-In
Held at Week 6. Replaces the normal Share with a brief group pulse. Not a retrospective—a mid-course correction opportunity. Allows for domain shifts, energy resets, and format adjustments if needed.

### Season Retrospective
Held at Week 12 (90–120 minutes). The most important gathering of the Season. The Circle asks eight specific questions together, celebrates achievements, extracts learnings, and decides the Circle's next chapter. The Scribe produces a brief Season Summary: key learnings, commitments kept and missed, and one improvement for next Season.

## Roles

### Facilitator
The person who holds the time and the space during a gathering. Opens and closes on time, ensures equal participation, protects The Open, keeps The Commit concrete, and names the next Facilitator. At Level 1, rotates weekly. At Level 2, rotates seasonally. Not a leader—a timekeeper and container-holder.

### Scribe
Activates at Level 2. Takes brief notes at each gathering: key topics covered, commitments made, decisions reached. Publishes notes to the group thread within 48 hours. Maintains the Season record (Opening intentions, mid-Season notes, weekly log, Retrospective summary). The notes belong to the Circle, not the Scribe. All members have permanent access.

### Steward
Activates at Level 2 when the Circle pools resources. Maintains a single, fully transparent ledger that every member can read at all times. Reports on shared resources at relevant gatherings. Cannot receive individual financial benefit from Circle assets. Coordinates logistics. Flags resource concerns immediately. Rotates each Season.

### Bridge
Activates at Level 2 when the Circle participates in a Mesh. Attends Mesh coordination gatherings seasonally and carries information in both directions. Has no authority to commit the Circle without full Circle consent. Reports back at the next gathering. Flags cross-Circle opportunities or conflicts for full Circle discussion. Rotates seasonally.

## Decision-Making

### Consent-Based Decision-Making
A proposal moves forward when no member raises a Valid Objection. This is not unanimity—it is the absence of genuine harm. Members do not need to love a proposal to consent to it. They just need to believe it won't cause harm to the Circle or its people.

### Valid Objection
A Valid Objection identifies specific, demonstrable harm the proposal would cause to the Circle or its members. Examples of what is NOT a Valid Objection: a preference for a different outcome, discomfort with change, or simply not liking something. If a Valid Objection is raised, the Circle works to modify the proposal to address that harm.

## Conflict & Escalation

### Module A: Scale Trigger
Activated when an 8th person requests to join a Circle. Seven is the cognitive ceiling for deep mutual accountability. Protocol: The Seeding Ceremony. The Circle undergoes a formal cell division into two independent Circles.

### Module B: Capital Trigger
Activated when a Circle chooses to pool money for any shared purpose. Protocol: The Steward and Transparent Ledger. Moment resources are shared, the potential for exploitation exists. This module protects against it through radical transparency.

### Module C: Friction Trigger
Activated when an internal conflict remains unresolved after two weekly gatherings. Protocol: Consent-Based Decision-Making and the Mesh. Unresolved conflict goes underground and becomes invisible culture. This module surfaces it before it settles.

### Seeding Ceremony
A formal four-step cell division when a Circle reaches 8+ members: (1) Final shared Retrospective, (2) Transparent resource division, (3) Formal naming of both Circles, (4) Mutual well-wishing. Neither Circle is "the original." Both carry the full DNA of the Framework forward.

## Anti-Cult Safeguards

### Red Flag Protocol
Every member is empowered—and expected—to name patterns if they appear. Naming is not betrayal; it is the mechanism that keeps the Circle honest. Red flags include: permanent authority figures, pressure to conform to beliefs, discouragement of outside relationships, judgment of questioners, financial pressure, suppression of dissent, superior/inferior language, difficulty leaving, indispensable entities, escalating commitment pressure, and us-versus-them language.

### Infrastructure Capture Shield
A constitutional hard rule: No single entity may own or control the framework's infrastructure. The registry of active Circles must be distributed across multiple independent hosts, independently forkable, operable without permission, and free to use. Whoever controls the directory controls the framework. The directory must therefore belong to no one—and everyone.

### Right to Fork
Any Circle may adapt the framework locally, publish improvements, and diverge from the original structure without permission. This is not disloyalty; it is the system working as designed. Significant divergences should be documented so members understand what they're joining.

### Right to Leave
Leaving a Circle carries no shame, no social debt, and no consequence. The door is always open in both directions. If leaving ever feels costly—socially, financially, or emotionally—the Circle has violated its foundational principles.

## Life Domains

Areas of life that Circles choose to focus on during a Season. No domain is mandatory. The group picks what matters most to them right now.

- **Financial Literacy:** Budgeting, debt, saving, investing, income diversification, financial independence basics.
- **Physical Health:** Movement, sleep, nutrition, rest, sustainable energy. Not performance optimization—sustainable wellbeing.
- **Mental & Emotional Health:** Stress, anxiety, emotional regulation, therapy access, self-awareness, nervous system basics.
- **Relationships:** Communication, conflict, intimacy, family dynamics, boundaries, loneliness, chosen family.
- **Philosophy & Ethics:** First-principles thinking, ethical reasoning through dialogue, examining assumptions, worldview.
- **Civic Engagement:** Local community, voting, service, neighborhood relationships, civic institutions, local government.
- **Creativity & Expression:** Art, music, writing, making—as practice, not performance. Includes play and leisure.
- **Career & Purpose:** Meaningful work, skills development, professional growth, navigating transitions.
- **Parenting & Family:** Raising children, caring for elders, family systems, intergenerational patterns.
- **Science & Critical Thinking:** How to evaluate evidence, understand systems, resist misinformation, think probabilistically.
- **Spirituality & Meaning:** Secular-compatible exploration of meaning, mortality, transcendence, and inner life. No doctrine.
- **Service & Contribution:** Giving back, volunteering, community projects, the ethics and practice of helping others well.

## Core Values

- **Truth-Seeking:** We pursue what is true, not what is comfortable. We revise our views when evidence demands it.
- **Compassion:** We always strive to empathize and extend genuine care to those around us—including those different from us and those we find difficult.
- **Accountability:** We keep our commitments or we say clearly when we can't.
- **Intellectual Humility:** We hold our certainties lightly. No idea is too important to question.
- **Nonviolence:** In action, language, and intent. We resolve conflict through dialogue, not force or coercion.
- **Stewardship:** We take responsibility for our resources, our community, and our local environment.
- **Continuous Learning:** Across every domain of life—body, mind, relationships, finances, community, spirit.
- **Service:** We contribute to something larger than ourselves. Belonging is not only received; it is built.
- **Joy:** Play, celebration, friendship, and rest are not optional. They are essential.
- **Simplicity:** If you cannot explain it to your neighbor in two minutes, simplify it.

---

## What COGS Is Not

- **Not a religion:** Makes no metaphysical claims. Does not require or prohibit any belief.
- **Not a political movement:** Takes no position on any party, candidate, or policy.
- **Not a self-help brand:** Does not sell anything.
- **Not a charity organization:** Is a community framework that, by choice, can extend outward toward those who need it.
- **Not a cult:** Has built its most important mechanisms specifically to prevent becoming one.
