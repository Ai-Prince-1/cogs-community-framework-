# COGS Framework Constitution

## Purpose
This document outlines the foundational governance principles, structural rules, and operational framework of the COGS (Circle of Good Stewards) community structure. It serves as the constitutional framework for how Circles, Meshes, and Assemblies operate.

---

## Part I: Foundational Principles

### 1. Non-Hierarchical Leadership
- No permanent leaders or gurus
- All leadership roles rotate on a defined schedule
- No individual holds authority over collective decisions
- Every member has equal standing and voice

### 2. Decentralized Decision-Making
- Decisions are made at the smallest competent scale
- Consent-based decision-making is the default protocol
- No central authority can override local Circle decisions
- The Mesh coordinates but does not govern
- The Assembly celebrates and learns but does not command

### 3. Practice Over Belief
- COGS is a working method, not a belief system
- Members are not required to adopt shared beliefs or ideology
- What members believe privately is entirely their own affair
- Participation requires only commitment to practice and honesty in reporting

### 4. Open-Source Governance
- The framework is forkable by any community
- Improvements are shared freely
- No central entity owns or controls the infrastructure
- The framework evolves through distributed learning, not top-down decree

### 5. Infrastructure Capture Shield (Hard Constitutional Rule)
**No single entity may own or control the framework's infrastructure.**

- The registry of active Circles must be distributed across multiple independent hosts
- The registry must be independently forkable by any community
- The registry must be operable without permission from any central body
- The registry must be free to use, copy, and maintain by anyone

*Whoever controls the directory controls the framework. The directory must therefore belong to no one — and everyone.*

## Part II: Structural Organization

### Tier 1: The Circle
**Scale:** 3–7 people
**Frequency:** Weekly gatherings (70 minutes)
**Duration:** Organized in 12-week Seasons

**Governance:**
- All members have equal standing
- Facilitator role rotates weekly
- Decisions made by simple discussion and informal agreement at Level 1
- Consent-based decision-making activates when disagreement arises
- The Circle is self-governing within the framework

**Accountability:**
- To each other for commitments made
- To the framework's core values
- To the Mesh (if participating) for shared agreements only

### Tier 2: The Mesh
**Scale:** Up to 7 Circles (~49 people)
**Frequency:** Bridge coordination meetings seasonally
**Purpose:** Community-layer coordination, shared learning, peer mediation

**Governance:**
- No permanent leadership; Bridge role rotates within each Circle seasonally
- Bridge delegates coordinate between Circles
- No Mesh decision can override a Circle decision
- No Bridge delegate can commit their Circle without full Circle consent
- The Mesh maintains a distributed registry of active Circles
- The Mesh provides neutral mediation when requested

**Authority Limits:**
- Cannot impose decisions on member Circles
- Cannot control Circle resources or governance
- Cannot require participation from Circles
- Cannot speak for or commit any Circle

### Tier 3: The Assembly
**Scale:** Up to 3 Meshes (~147 people)
**Frequency:** Annual convening or when cross-Mesh challenges arise
**Purpose:** Tribal-layer celebration, cross-Mesh learning, collective reflection

**Governance:**
- No authority over any Mesh or Circle
- Convenes for celebration, learning, and coordination only
- Cannot make binding decisions affecting Circles or Meshes
- Encourages diversity across cultural, ethnic, and socioeconomic lines

## Part III: Level 1 Operations (Core, Zero-Friction)

### The Weekly Gathering (70 minutes)
**Non-negotiable Structure:**
- **10 min | The Open:** Check-in round. One sentence each. No advice, no responses. Listening only.
- **45 min | The Share:** Each member reports on past week in active domains. What was tried, what worked, what failed, what was learned.
- **15 min | The Commit:** Each member names one specific, self-chosen action for the coming week.
- **Close | The Hand-Off:** Current Facilitator names next week's Facilitator. Gathering ends on time.

### The Facilitator Role (Level 1)
**Rotation:** Weekly. Every member facilitates before anyone facilitates twice.

**Responsibilities:**
- Open and close the gathering precisely on time
- Ensure equal time in The Share
- Protect The Open (no advice, responses, or cross-talk)
- Keep The Commit concrete and specific
- Name the next Facilitator explicitly

**Authority:**
- None beyond holding the structure
- Cannot make unilateral decisions
- Cannot override group agreements

### The Accountability Ledger
- Each member posts their weekly commitment to the group's shared thread within one hour of gathering
- Format: Domain + specific action
- The following week, The Share begins with reporting on previous week's commitment
- This replaces the need for a Scribe at Level 1

### The Season (12 weeks)
**Season Opening (90-120 minutes):**
- Check-in round
- Domain selection (2–3 domains chosen by group)
- Personal intentions stated by each member
- Facilitator rotation established
- Resource conversation held
- Outward support conversation held

**Mid-Season Check-in (Week 6):**
- Brief group pulse
- Mid-course corrections allowed
- Domains can shift if needed

**Season Retrospective (90-120 minutes, Week 12):**
- Eight retrospective questions asked and answered
- Season Summary produced by Scribe (or designated member)
- Role rotation confirmed
- Circle decides: continue, pause, or close
- Celebration and acknowledgment

## Part IV: Level 2 Governance Modules

Level 2 modules are **protections**, not requirements. They activate only when specific real-world conditions are met.

### Module A: The Scale Trigger
**Activation:** A Circle receives a request from an 8th person to join.

**Rationale:** Seven is the cognitive ceiling for deep mutual accountability without administrative overhead.

**Protocol: The Seeding Ceremony**
1. Hold a final shared Retrospective honoring what was built together
2. Transparently divide any shared resources, records, or commitments
3. Formally name each new Circle
4. Mutual well-wishing: *"We carry the same values forward, differently expressed"*

**Outcome:** Two independent Circles, each carrying the full DNA of the COGS Framework forward. Neither is "the original."

### Module B: The Capital Trigger
**Activation:** A Circle chooses to pool money for any shared purpose.

**Rationale:** When resources are shared, the potential for exploitation exists. This module protects against it through radical transparency.

**Protocol: The Steward and Transparent Ledger**
- The Circle designates a formal Steward for the Season
- The Steward maintains a single, fully transparent ledger (read access for all members at all times)
- All contributions remain voluntary (no compulsory amounts, no tithing)
- The Steward may not receive individual financial benefit from shared assets
- Every financial decision requires full Circle consent
- The Steward role rotates each Season
- The ledger remains accessible to all members after the Steward's role ends

### Module C: The Friction Trigger
**Activation:** An internal conflict remains unresolved after two weekly gatherings.

**Rationale:** Unresolved conflict goes underground and becomes invisible culture. This module surfaces it before it settles.

**Protocol: Consent-Based Decision-Making and the Mesh**
- The Circle formally adopts consent-based decision-making for conflict-related decisions
- A Valid Objection = specific, demonstrable harm to the Circle or its members
- Personal preference is NOT a Valid Objection
- The Facilitator structures a dedicated conflict conversation within the next gathering
- If internal mediation fails, the Bridge requests a neutral Circle from the Mesh to assist
- If Mesh mediation also fails, the Seeding Ceremony is available as a dignified path to separation

## Part V: Anti-Cult Safeguards

### Built-In Self-Correction Mechanisms

**1. The Red Flag Protocol**
Every member is empowered—and expected—to name these patterns immediately:
- Any member being treated as a permanent authority or moral superior
- Pressure to conform to beliefs, ideologies, or worldviews
- Members being discouraged from outside relationships or perspectives
- Judgment or social cost for questioning the framework
- Financial pressure, compulsory contributions, or personal enrichment
- Suppression of honest dissent or disagreement
- Language positioning members as superior to non-members
- Difficulty or shame around leaving
- A platform or entity becoming indispensable to the framework's survival
- Escalating commitment pressure — "real members do X"
- Us-versus-them language about people outside the Circle

**Response Protocol:**
1. Say it directly at any gathering: "I want to name something I've been observing"
2. The Facilitator pauses the agenda and creates space
3. The Circle listens without interruption or defense
4. The group discusses and decides how to address it
5. If the pattern persists, any member may exit or fork without shame

**2. The Retrospective as Immune System**
The Season Retrospective is the framework's primary self-correction mechanism. The eight retrospective questions are specifically designed to surface exactly the patterns that lead to harm:
1. What genuinely worked this Season? What is the evidence?
2. What didn't work, and why? What did we avoid talking about?
3. Are we becoming more open or more closed? Be specific.
4. Are all voices genuinely heard? Who spoke least? Why?
5. Are members healthier, wiser, more connected than 12 weeks ago?
6. Is anything about how we operate worth changing for next Season?
7. Are there any signs of the warning patterns from the Manifesto?
8. What do we want to carry forward? What do we leave behind?

**Structural Certainty:** A Circle that skips Retrospectives will drift. This is not a prediction—it is structural inevitability. The Retrospective is not optional.

**3. The Right to Leave**
- Leaving carries no shame, no social debt, no consequence
- The door is always open in both directions
- If leaving ever feels costly—socially, financially, or emotionally—the Circle has violated its foundational principles

**4. The Right to Fork**
- Any Circle may adapt the framework locally, publish improvements, and diverge without permission
- This is not disloyalty; it is the system working as designed
- Significant divergence should be documented so members understand what they're joining

## Part VI: Rights and Responsibilities

### Member Rights
- Equal voice in Circle governance
- Right to honest feedback and peer support
- Right to set boundaries
- Right to request what you need
- Right to leave at any time without consequence
- Right to rest and sabbaticals
- Right to challenge the framework
- Right to request outside mediation

### Member Responsibilities
- Show up when you commit to showing up
- Be honest about your actual experience, not the presentable version
- Listen without judgment
- Honor others' boundaries
- Keep commitments or state clearly when you can't
- Contribute to the Circle's health
- Name patterns when you see them
- Respect the framework's operating structure

### Circle Collective Responsibilities
- Maintain the weekly gathering structure
- Hold retrospectives every Season
- Rotate leadership roles
- Make decisions by consent
- Handle conflict at the smallest possible scale first
- Remain accountable to one another
- Protect the dignity and wellbeing of all members
- Stay alert to warning patterns

---

## Part VII: Conflicts and Escalation

### Three-Level Conflict Protocol

**Level 1: Direct Conversation**
- The two people involved speak privately, with goodwill, before the next gathering
- Most conflicts resolve here

**Level 2: Full Circle Mediation**
- If unresolved after two gatherings, Module C activates
- The Facilitator structures a dedicated conversation
- Consent-based resolution attempted

**Level 3: Mesh Mediation**
- If still unresolved, the Bridge requests a neutral Circle from the Mesh to assist
- External perspective and facilitation

### Core Principle
Name it early. The longer a conflict goes unnamed, the more it becomes the invisible architecture of the group—shaping everything without anyone being able to address it directly.

---

## Part VIII: Amendments and Evolution

### Amendment Process
1. Any Circle may propose changes to the framework
2. Proposals are shared with the broader community (via Mesh/Assembly)
3. Changes are tested locally before broader adoption
4. Successful innovations are documented and shared
5. The framework evolves through distributed learning, not central decree

### Version Control
- Framework versions are numbered and documented
- Breaking changes are noted clearly
- Historical versions remain available
- No version is considered "final truth"

---

## Part IX: Constitutional Hard Rules

These rules are non-negotiable and apply to all Circles, Meshes, and Assemblies:

1. **No permanent leaders.** All leadership roles must rotate.
2. **No compulsory financial contributions.** All contributions must be voluntary.
3. **No doctrine required.** Members are not required to adopt shared beliefs.
4. **No centralized control.** No entity may own or exclusively control the framework's infrastructure.
5. **Consent-based decisions on consequential matters.** No single person can make binding decisions affecting the whole Circle.
6. **Right to leave.** Leaving must carry no consequence, shame, or social debt.
7. **Retrospectives are mandatory.** Circles that skip Retrospectives violate this constitution.
8. **Anti-cult protocols are built-in.** Red Flag Protocol must be available and used if patterns emerge.

---

## Final Principle

This constitution is not final truth. It is an evolving experiment. It will be wrong about some things. It will miss things. It will need revision.

If it ever becomes rigid, authoritarian, identity-driven, or resistant to honest challenge, it has violated its own foundational principles.

**Revise it. Fork it. If necessary, abandon it.**

The goal was never the framework. The goal was the people.
