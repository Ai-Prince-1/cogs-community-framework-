### How to use this handbook

*   **The Manifesto** tells you why COGS exists.
*   **This Handbook** tells you how to run it.

You do not need to read this cover to cover before starting. Read Section 1 to start your first Circle today. Return to the rest as your Circle grows and needs arise.

## — Section 1  ·  Starting a Circle —

### How to start your first Circle
You need three things to start a Circle: a small group of people who trust each other enough to show up honestly, a shared commitment to 70 minutes a week, and this handbook.

That's it. No budget. No application. No permission from anyone.

#### Step 1 — Find your people
Start with people you already know and trust — friends, neighbors, colleagues, family. You are not recruiting for a movement. You are inviting people into a practice.

*   **Ideal founding Circle:** 4 to 6 people.
*   **Minimum:** Three is enough to begin.
*   **Maximum:** Seven is the ceiling. Do not exceed seven — when an eighth person wants to join, the Seeding Ceremony activates (see Section 7).

**Who makes a good founding member:**
*   Someone who shows up when they say they will.
*   Someone willing to be honest about their actual life, not just the presentable version.
*   Someone who can disagree without withdrawing.
*   Someone curious enough to keep learning.

> ### Who to avoid in a founding Circle
> *Not a personality judgment — a structural one:*
> *   People who consistently cancel at the last minute
> *   People who cannot tolerate being questioned
> *   People who need to be the authority in every room
> *   People in active, unmanaged crisis that requires professional support (*COGS is peer support, not clinical care*)

#### Step 2 — Have the first conversation
Before your first formal gathering, have one informal conversation with your group. Share the Manifesto or describe the framework in your own words. Let people ask questions.

> ### How to invite someone
> "I'm starting a small group — six people, meeting weekly for 70 minutes. We pick a couple of areas to work on together each season: things like finances, health, relationships, what we're learning. Everyone commits to showing up and being honest. No judgment, no fixing each other — just real conversation and mutual support. I think you'd be good in this. Are you interested?"

#### Step 3 — Hold your Season Opening
The Season Opening is your first formal gathering. It sets the tone, the domains, and the commitments for the next 12 weeks. Run it using the Season Opening Checklist in Section 11.

### What a Circle is not responsible for
Being clear about this from the start prevents scope creep and burnout:
*   **A Circle is not a therapy group.** It provides peer support, not clinical care.
*   **A Circle is not a financial advisory service.** It builds financial literacy together, not professional advice.
*   **A Circle is not a crisis hotline.** If a member is in acute crisis, the Circle's role is to help them access professional support — not to manage the crisis itself.
*   **A Circle is not a social media community.** Its strength comes from in-person or live connection, not asynchronous posting.

## — Section 2  ·  The weekly gathering —

### Running the 70-minute weekly gathering
Every gathering follows the same structure. This is not rigidity — it is the structure that creates the safety for honest conversation. When people know what to expect, they can relax into being present.

#### The agenda — locked at 70 minutes

| Time | Phase | What happens |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **10 min** | **The Open** | Check-in round. One sentence each: *how are you arriving today?* No advice, no responses. Just listening. |
| **45 min** | **The Share** | Each member reports on the past week in the active seasonal domains. What did you try? What worked? What failed? What did you learn? |
| **15 min** | **The Commit** | Each member names one specific, self-chosen action for the coming week. Concrete. Measurable. Achievable by next gathering. |
| **Close** | **The Hand-Off** | Current Facilitator names next week's Facilitator. Gathering ends on time. |

### The Facilitator's role
At Level 1, the Facilitator rotates weekly. Every member facilitates before anyone facilitates twice. The role is light:
*   Open and close the gathering on time.
*   Give everyone equal time in The Share.
*   Stop advice-giving during The Open — that phase is for listening only.
*   Keep The Commit concrete — push back gently on vague intentions.
*   Name the next Facilitator at the close.

> ### What the Facilitator does NOT do
> *   Interpret, evaluate, or comment on what members share
> *   Give advice unless specifically asked in The Share
> *   Dominate the conversation
> *   Skip The Hand-Off — this is how rotation stays alive

### The accountability ledger
Immediately after every gathering — within the same hour if possible — each member posts their weekly commitment to the group's shared thread. This is the **Zero-Overhead Ledger**: a living record of what people committed to and whether they followed through.

> ### Commitment post format
> **Domain:** [Financial Literacy]
> **My action this week:** I will review my last 3 months of bank statements and identify my top 3 non-essential recurring costs by Thursday.

Keep it specific. Keep it achievable. Keep it honest.

The following week, The Share begins with each member reporting on their commitment from the previous week — done, not done, or partially done and here's what happened. No judgment. No lectures. Just honest accounting.

### When a member misses a gathering
Missing happens. The protocol is simple:
1.  The absent member reads the group thread to catch up.
2.  They post their weekly commitment to the thread as normal.
3.  If someone misses three consecutive gatherings without communication, the Facilitator checks in privately before the next session.

### Online vs. in-person gatherings
In-person is strongly preferred. Physical co-presence creates a quality of attention and trust that video cannot fully replicate. That said, life is real — some Circles will run online, and that is legitimate.

**If running online:**
*   **Video on** for the full 70 minutes. Cameras off is equivalent to not showing up.
*   **Use a stable, quiet environment.** Driving or multitasking disrespects the group.
*   **The Open is more important remotely**, not less — it creates the presence that the room would otherwise provide.

## — Section 3  ·  The Season —

### Running a 12-week Season
A Season is a 12-week focused cycle. It has a clear beginning, middle, and end. The structure keeps the Circle moving forward rather than drifting.

#### The Season Opening — Week 1
The Season Opening is the one gathering per Season that is longer than 70 minutes. Allow 90 to 120 minutes. It sets everything that follows.

| Agenda item | Time | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Check-in round** | 15 min | How is everyone arriving into this new Season? |
| **Domain selection** | 20 min | Vote on 2–3 life domains to focus on this Season (see domain list below). |
| **Personal intentions** | 20 min | Each member states one personal intention for the Season — not a goal, a direction. |
| **Role assignments** | 15 min | Assign Facilitator order for the coming weeks. At Level 2: assign Steward, Scribe, Bridge. |
| **Resource conversation** | 10 min | Does the Circle want to pool anything this Season? (Level 2 Module B activates if yes.) |
| **Outward support (optional)** | 10 min | Does the Circle have capacity to support someone outside the group this Season? |
| **Close** | 10 min | Scribe reads back all intentions and commitments. First weekly Facilitator confirmed. |

#### Life domains — choose 2 to 3 per Season
Circles choose their domains together. No domain is mandatory. The group picks what matters most to them right now.

| Domain | What it covers |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Financial literacy** | Budgeting, debt, saving, investing, income diversification, financial independence basics. |
| **Physical health** | Movement, sleep, nutrition, rest, sustainable energy. Not performance optimization — sustainable wellbeing. |
| **Mental & emotional health** | Stress, anxiety, emotional regulation, therapy access, self-awareness, nervous system basics. |
| **Relationships** | Communication, conflict, intimacy, family dynamics, boundaries, loneliness, chosen family. |
| **Philosophy & ethics** | First-principles thinking, ethical reasoning through dialogue, examining assumptions, worldview. |
| **Civic engagement** | Local community, voting, service, neighborhood relationships, civic institutions, local government. |
| **Creativity & expression** | Art, music, writing, making — as practice, not performance. Includes play and leisure. |
| **Career & purpose** | Meaningful work, skills development, professional growth, navigating transitions. |
| **Parenting & family** | Raising children, caring for elders, family systems, intergenerational patterns. |
| **Science & critical thinking** | How to evaluate evidence, understand systems, resist misinformation, think probabilistically. |
| **Spirituality & meaning** | Secular-compatible exploration of meaning, mortality, transcendence, and inner life. No doctrine. |
| **Service & contribution** | Giving back, volunteering, community projects, the ethics and practice of helping others well. |

### The mid-Season check-in — Week 6
At week 6, replace the normal Share with a brief group pulse. This is not a retrospective — it is a mid-course correction opportunity:
*   Are the domains still relevant? Has something more urgent emerged?
*   Is everyone's energy holding? Any signs of burnout or withdrawal?
*   Is the weekly commitment format working? Too easy, too hard, or right?
*   Is anyone feeling unheard or underserved by the current format?

If something needs changing, change it. A Season is a living cycle, not a locked contract.

### The Season Retrospective — Week 12
The Retrospective is the most important gathering of the Season. Allow 90 to 120 minutes. This is where the Circle self-corrects, celebrates, and sets up the next Season.

#### Retrospective questions — the Circle asks these together
1.  What genuinely worked this Season? What is the evidence?
2.  What didn't work, and why? What did we avoid talking about?
3.  Are we becoming more open or more closed? Be specific.
4.  Are all voices genuinely heard? Who spoke least? Why?
5.  Are members healthier, wiser, more connected than 12 weeks ago?
6.  Is anything about how we operate worth changing for next Season?
7.  Are there any signs of the warning patterns from the Manifesto?
8.  What do we want to carry forward? What do we leave behind?

After the Retrospective, the Scribe produces a brief **Season Summary**: key learnings, commitments kept and missed, and one improvement for next Season. This becomes part of the Circle's permanent record.

Role rotation happens at the Retrospective. New roles for Level 2 Circles are confirmed before the next Season Opening.

## — Section 4  ·  Rotating roles —

### The four rotating roles
At Level 1, the only rotating role is the weekly Facilitator. The other three roles activate at Level 2 — when the Circle is managing shared resources, navigating persistent conflict, or participating in a Mesh.

#### 1. The Facilitator
*Rotates: weekly at Level 1, seasonally at Level 2.*

| Responsibility | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Open and close on time** | The gathering starts when you say it does and ends at 70 minutes. No exceptions without group consent. |
| **Equal time in The Share** | Watch the clock. Redistribute time if one member is consistently over. |
| **Protect The Open** | No advice, no responses, no cross-talk during check-in. Listen only. |
| **Keep The Commit concrete** | "I'll try to be healthier" is not a commitment. "I will walk 30 minutes on Monday, Wednesday, Friday" is. |
| **Hand off clearly** | Name the next Facilitator explicitly before closing. Don't let it go unnamed. |

> ### First-time Facilitator guidance
> *   Nervous is normal. The structure does most of the work for you.
> *   Your job is not to be impressive — it is to hold the time and the space.
> *   If something goes sideways, name it simply and move on.
> *   The group will support you. That's the whole point.

#### 2. The Steward (Level 2 — Module B)
*Rotates: seasonally. Activates when the Circle pools resources.*
*   Maintains the Circle's transparent resource ledger — every member has permanent read access.
*   Reports on shared resources at every gathering where they are relevant.
*   Cannot receive individual financial benefit from the Circle's shared assets.
*   Coordinates logistics: venue, tools, any shared materials.
*   Flags any resource concern to the full Circle immediately — no unilateral decisions.

> ### The Steward is not a treasurer with discretion
> Every financial decision made with shared Circle resources requires full Circle consent. The Steward executes decisions — does not make them.

#### 3. The Scribe (Level 2)
*Rotates: seasonally. Maintains the Circle's living record.*
*   Takes brief notes at each gathering: key topics covered, commitments made, any decisions reached.
*   Publishes notes to the group thread within 48 hours of each gathering.
*   Maintains the Season record: Opening intentions, mid-Season check-in notes, weekly commitments log, and Retrospective summary.
*   The notes belong to the Circle, not the Scribe. They are accessible to all members at all times.

*Note: At Level 1, the accountability ledger (each member's commitment post to the group thread) replaces the formal Scribe function. The Scribe role activates when the Circle decides to move to Level 2.*

#### 4. The Bridge (Level 2 — Mesh participation)
*Rotates: seasonally. Represents the Circle at Mesh coordination.*
*   Attends Mesh coordination gatherings and carries information in both directions.
*   Has no authority to commit the Circle to anything without full Circle consent.
*   Reports back to the Circle after every Mesh interaction — within the next gathering at the latest.
*   Flags any cross-Circle issue or opportunity that the full Circle should discuss.

### Role rotation protocol
At the Season Retrospective, roles rotate. No one keeps the same role twice in a row. If someone strongly prefers not to take a role, they may defer once. A second consecutive deferral moves to the full Circle for discussion.

## — Section 5  ·  Decisions, conflict, and difficult conversations —

### How Circles make decisions
At Level 1, most decisions are made by simple discussion and informal agreement. When disagreement arises or when a decision is consequential, the Circle uses consent-based decision making (see Module C for the full protocol).

#### Consent-based decision making
A proposal moves forward when no member raises a **Valid Objection**. This is not unanimity — it is the absence of genuine harm. Members do not need to love a proposal to consent to it. They just need to believe it won't cause harm to the Circle or its people.

> ### What is a Valid Objection?
> A Valid Objection identifies specific, demonstrable harm the proposal would cause to the Circle or its members.
>
> *   A preference for a different outcome is **NOT** a Valid Objection.
> *   Discomfort with change is **NOT** a Valid Objection.
> *   "I just don't like it" is **NOT** a Valid Objection.
>
> If you have a Valid Objection, state the specific harm clearly. The Circle then works to modify the proposal to address that harm.

If a proposal cannot be modified to resolve all Valid Objections, it is set aside for the current Season and revisited at the Retrospective.

### Handling conflict — the three-level protocol
Conflict is handled at the smallest possible scale. Most conflicts should never need to leave Level 1.

| Level | When | How |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Direct conversation** | First instance | The two people involved speak privately, with goodwill, before the next gathering. |
| **2. Full Circle mediation** | Unresolved after 2 gatherings | Module C activates. The Facilitator structures a dedicated conversation. Consent-based resolution. |
| **3. Mesh mediation** | Still unresolved | Bridge requests a neutral Circle from the Mesh to assist as a third party. |

> ### The single most important conflict principle
> Name it early. The longer a conflict goes unnamed, the more it becomes the invisible architecture of the group — shaping everything without anyone being able to address it directly.
>
> The Facilitator is empowered to name tension they observe, even when no one else has spoken it. That is part of the role.

### Difficult conversations — practical guidance
Some conversations are hard regardless of how skilled the group is. These patterns help:
*   **Start with observation, not judgment:** "I noticed…" not "You always…"
*   **Separate the person from the pattern:** Behavior can be addressed without attacking character.
*   **Name the impact before proposing a solution:** Let the other person understand what you experienced before moving to fix it.
*   **Ask before advising:** "Would it be helpful if I shared what I think, or do you just need to be heard right now?"
*   **Close the loop:** A conversation isn't finished when it ends — it's finished when both people feel genuinely heard.

### When a member needs to leave
Leaving a Circle is always clean and unconditional. There is no exit process that requires permission, explanation, or approval. A member who wants to leave says so to the group at any gathering, or simply to the Facilitator in a private message.

If a member disappears without communication for more than three weeks, the Facilitator reaches out privately — once, with warmth and no pressure. The message is simply: *"We've missed you. If you're done or need a break, that's completely okay. We wanted you to know."*

## — Section 6  ·  The Mesh and the Assembly —

### Coordinating beyond a single Circle
A Circle that is running well will eventually want to connect with other Circles — to share learning, tackle larger community projects, or access mediation support. This is the Mesh.

#### Forming a Mesh
A Mesh forms when 2 or more Circles — up to a maximum of 7 — choose to coordinate. There is no minimum number of Seasons a Circle must complete before joining a Mesh, but in practice, most Circles stabilize first.

**How a Mesh forms:**
1.  Two or more Circle Bridges make contact — either organically or through the distributed Circle registry.
2.  The groups meet informally to assess compatibility of values and practical fit.
3.  If there is mutual interest, the Circles hold a joint Season Opening that includes Mesh coordination as a shared intention.
4.  The Mesh has no formal founding ceremony — it exists when Circles are actively coordinating through their Bridge delegates.

#### How the Mesh operates
*   Bridge delegates meet seasonally — typically once per Season outside of regular Circle gatherings.
*   The Mesh has no permanent leadership. The Bridge role rotates within each Circle seasonally.
*   The Mesh can coordinate joint service projects, shared learning events, and cross-Circle celebrations.
*   The Mesh maintains a distributed registry of active Circles — no single Circle or entity owns it.
*   The Mesh provides neutral mediation when a Circle requests it for persistent internal conflict.

> ### The Mesh does not govern Circles
> No Mesh decision overrides a Circle decision. No Bridge delegate can commit their Circle without full Circle consent. The Mesh coordinates. It does not command.

### The Assembly
When 2 or more Meshes exist in a region, they may form an Assembly — up to a maximum of 3 Meshes (~147 people). The Assembly convenes once a year, or when a challenge arises that no single Mesh can solve alone.

**The Assembly is a space for:**
*   Shared celebration and recognition of what the community has built.
*   Cross-Mesh learning — sharing what has worked across different Circles and contexts.
*   Collective reflection on the direction and health of the broader community.
*   Addressing challenges that span multiple Meshes.

The Assembly holds no authority over any Mesh or Circle. It coordinates and celebrates. Where it brings together Circles from different backgrounds, cultures, and communities, it also models the possibility of radically distributed leadership.

### The distributed registry — Infrastructure Capture Shield in practice
Every Mesh maintains a simple registry of active Circles in its network. This registry must be:
*   Accessible to every Circle member at all times.
*   Stored in at least two independent locations (e.g., a shared document, a public repository, a printed list).
*   Forkable — any Circle may copy and maintain their own version.
*   Not owned or exclusively controlled by any single person, Circle, or platform.

This is not bureaucracy. It is the practical expression of the Infrastructure Capture Shield: no single entity can cut off a Circle's access to the community it belongs to.

## — Section 7  ·  Level 2 governance modules —

### When and how to activate Level 2
Level 1 runs on trust and simplicity. Most Circles will run on Level 1 indefinitely. Level 2 modules are protections — they activate when specific real-world conditions arise, not before.

### Module A — The Scale Trigger
**Trigger:** *A Circle receives a request from an 8th person to join.*

> Seven is the cognitive ceiling for deep mutual accountability without administrative overhead. At 8, the Circle either protects its quality or divides into two groups that can each operate at full depth.

#### Protocol: The Seeding Ceremony

*   **Step 1 — Final Retrospective:** Hold a final shared Retrospective honoring what was built together. This is not a goodbye — it is a foundation.
*   **Step 2 — Resource division:** Transparently divide any shared resources, records, or commitments. Every member is present for this conversation. No unilateral decisions.
*   **Step 3 — Formal naming:** Each new Circle is formally named by its members. Both carry the full COGS values forward.
*   **Step 4 — Mutual well-wishing:** A spoken acknowledgment from both groups: *we carry the same values forward, differently expressed.* Neither Circle is the original. Both are equally legitimate.

The Seeding Ceremony is not a divorce. It is a cell division. Both groups carry the full DNA of The COGS Framework forward.

### Module B — The Capital Trigger
**Trigger:** *A Circle chooses to pool money for any shared purpose.*

> The moment resources are shared, the potential for exploitation exists. This module exists to make that impossible through radical transparency.

#### Protocol: The Steward and the Transparent Ledger
*   The Circle designates a formal Steward for the Season.
*   The Steward maintains a single transparent ledger that every member can read at all times.
*   All contributions are voluntary. No compulsory amounts. No tithing.
*   The Steward may not receive individual financial benefit from the Circle's shared assets.
*   Every financial decision requires full Circle consent.
*   The Steward role rotates each Season.
*   The ledger remains accessible to all members even after the Steward's role ends.

### Module C — The Friction Trigger
**Trigger:** *An internal conflict remains unresolved after two gatherings.*

> Unresolved conflict does not disappear — it goes underground and becomes the invisible culture of the group. This module surfaces it before it settles.

#### Protocol: Consent-Based Decisions and the Mesh
*   The Circle formally adopts consent-based decision making for the duration of the conflict and any affected decisions.
*   A Valid Objection = specific demonstrable harm to the Circle. Personal preference is not a Valid Objection.
*   The Facilitator structures a dedicated conflict conversation within the next gathering.
*   If internal mediation fails, the Bridge requests a neutral Circle from the Mesh to assist as a third party.
*   If Mesh mediation also fails, the Seeding Ceremony is available as a dignified path to separation.

## — Section 8  ·  Anti-cult and self-correction protocols —

### Keeping the Circle healthy
The COGS Framework has been designed with specific mechanisms to prevent the dynamics that turn good communities into harmful ones. This section makes those mechanisms explicit and actionable.

### The Red Flag Protocol
Every member of a Circle is empowered — and expected — to name these patterns if they appear. Naming is not betrayal. It is the mechanism that keeps the Circle honest.

> ### Red flags — name these immediately if you see them
> *   Any member being treated as a permanent authority or moral superior
> *   Pressure to conform to beliefs, ideologies, or worldviews
> *   Members being discouraged from outside relationships or perspectives
> *   Judgment or social cost for questioning the framework
> *   Financial pressure, compulsory contributions, or personal enrichment
> *   Suppression of honest dissent or disagreement
> *   Language positioning members as superior to non-members
> *   Difficulty or shame around leaving
> *   A platform or entity becoming indispensable to the framework's survival
> *   Escalating commitment pressure — "real members do X"
> *   Us-versus-them language about people outside the Circle

**How to raise a flag:**
1.  Say it directly at any gathering: "I want to name something I've been observing."
2.  The Facilitator pauses the agenda and creates space.
3.  The Circle listens without interruption or defense.
4.  The group discusses and decides how to address it.
5.  If the pattern persists after being named, any member may exit or fork without shame.

### The Retrospective as immune system
The Season Retrospective is the framework's primary self-correction mechanism. The eight retrospective questions (Section 3) are specifically designed to surface exactly the patterns that lead to harm.

A Circle that skips Retrospectives will drift. This is not a prediction — it is a structural certainty. The retrospective is not optional.

### The right to fork
Any Circle may adapt the framework locally, publish improvements, and diverge from the original structure without permission. This is not disloyalty. It is the system working as designed.

If a Circle diverges significantly from the core framework, it should note this clearly in its own documentation so that members and potential new members understand what they are joining.

## — Section 9  ·  Lightweight rituals —

### Rituals that build belonging without doctrine
Humans create ritual naturally. If you do not design lightweight rituals intentionally, the group will create them unconsciously — and unconscious rituals can carry meaning that wasn't intended.

The rituals below are offered as options, not requirements. They are non-mystical, non-doctrinal, and immediately adaptable to any cultural context. Take what fits. Leave what doesn't.

#### Opening rituals
*   **A moment of silence (30 seconds)** before The Open begins — a transition from wherever people came from to the present room.
*   **A shared meal or snack** before the gathering — the act of eating together is one of humanity's oldest trust-builders.
*   **A single opening question** posed by the Facilitator — something beyond "how are you": *"What's something you noticed this week that surprised you?"*

#### Season rituals
*   **Season Opening:** each member writes their intention on a card and keeps it until the Retrospective.
*   **Season Retrospective:** each member reads their original intention aloud and reflects on where they arrived.
*   **A shared meal** at the Season Opening and Retrospective — marking the beginning and end of a cycle.

#### Welcoming a new member
*   Each existing member says one sentence about why they are glad to be in this Circle — not directed at the new member, but heard by them.
*   The new member is given time to share what brought them here, without interruption.
*   The Circle acknowledges the new member's presence by name before moving to the agenda.

#### Marking exits
*   When a member leaves, the Circle takes five minutes to acknowledge what they contributed.
*   No shame, no analysis of why they left, no guilt — just honest recognition of what they brought.

#### Grief and difficulty
*   When a member is going through genuine difficulty — loss, illness, crisis — the Circle's first response is presence, not advice. The Open expands to give the person space.

## — Section 10  ·  Outward support — the optional extension —

### Reaching beyond the Circle
The primary purpose of a Circle is the mutual growth, support, and belonging of its own members. This is enough. This is more than most people have.

The best contribution most people can make to their wider community is to become genuinely well themselves. A Circle of seven people who are healthier, wiser, more financially stable, more connected, and more capable of honest conversation is a gift to the world.

Some Circles, when they have genuine capacity, choose to extend support outward — collectively helping one person outside the group who is navigating a genuine season of difficulty: housing insecurity, job loss, recovery, grief, or major life transition.

#### If a Circle chooses outward support
*   The decision is made by full Circle consent — not majority vote, not quiet assumption. Every member genuinely agrees.
*   Module B activates if any financial contribution is involved.
*   The person being supported is treated as a future peer, not a charity case. Their dignity is non-negotiable.
*   The Circle defines clearly what support it can offer and for how long. Boundaries set in advance prevent resentment.
*   The goal is always restored capacity, not ongoing dependency.
*   When the person is stable and thriving, the Circle's role formally closes. They are invited — never pressured — to join or seed a Circle of their own.

> ### What outward support is not
> *   Professional clinical care, therapy, or medical support
> *   Financial advising or legal assistance
> *   Crisis management for acute mental health emergencies
> *   A permanent support arrangement without defined boundaries
>
> When someone needs professional support, the Circle's role is to help them access it — not to substitute for it.

## — Section 11  ·  Quick-start checklists —

### Checklists for every occasion
*Print these, share them, adapt them. Each one is designed to be used in the moment, not just read in advance.*

#### 1. Starting your first Circle
* [ ] Identified 3–6 founding members
* [ ] Had the initial informal conversation — everyone has genuinely chosen to join
* [ ] Agreed on a regular meeting time and format (in-person / online)
* [ ] Set up a shared communication thread (WhatsApp, Signal, or equivalent)
* [ ] Scheduled the Season Opening (allow 90–120 minutes)
* [ ] Shared the Manifesto or summarized COGS in your own words

#### 2. Season Opening checklist
* [ ] Check-in round complete (15 min)
* [ ] 2–3 life domains selected by group consensus
* [ ] Each member has stated a personal Season intention
* [ ] Weekly Facilitator order confirmed for the Season
* [ ] Level 2 roles assigned if applicable (Steward, Scribe, Bridge)
* [ ] Resource conversation held — decision made (pool or not)
* [ ] Outward support conversation held — decision made (yes or no, this Season)
* [ ] Scribe has read back all intentions and commitments
* [ ] First weekly gathering date confirmed

#### 3. Weekly gathering checklist
* [ ] Facilitator confirmed and prepared
* [ ] **The Open:** check-in round complete (10 min)
* [ ] **The Share:** all members reported on the week (45 min)
* [ ] **The Commit:** all members have named a specific action (15 min)
* [ ] **The Hand-Off:** next Facilitator named
* [ ] All members post commitments to group thread within the hour

#### 4. Season Retrospective checklist
* [ ] All 8 retrospective questions addressed
* [ ] Red Flag Protocol reviewed — any patterns to name?
* [ ] Season Summary drafted by Scribe
* [ ] Role rotation confirmed for next Season
* [ ] New Season Opening scheduled
* [ ] Any celebration or acknowledgment of the Season completed

#### 5. Seeding Ceremony checklist
* [ ] Final shared Retrospective held and complete
* [ ] All shared resources transparently divided and agreed by all members
* [ ] Both new Circles formally named
* [ ] Mutual well-wishing spoken by both groups
* [ ] Both Circles have confirmed their first Season Opening date
* [ ] Registry updated to reflect the two new Circles

## — Section 12  ·  Role playbooks — one-page guides —

### Role playbooks
*These one-page guides are designed to be handed to a member at the moment they take on a role — not studied in advance, but used in the moment.*

#### Facilitator playbook
> **Your job in one sentence:** Hold the time and the space.

*   **Before the gathering:**
    *   Confirm the gathering time and send a brief reminder to the group.
    *   Review last week's commitments from the group thread.
*   **During The Open (10 min):**
    *   Open with: "Let's begin. One sentence each — how are you arriving today?"
    *   No advice, no responses. Just hold space and listen.
    *   Keep it to one sentence per person. Time it.
*   **During The Share (45 min):**
    *   Invite the first person: "[Name], would you like to start?"
    *   Watch the clock. Redistribute gently if someone runs long.
    *   Encourage honest reporting: tries, wins, failures, learnings.
*   **During The Commit (15 min):**
    *   Ask each person: "What is your specific action for this week?"
    *   Push back on vague intentions: "Can you make that more specific?"
*   **At The Hand-Off:**
    *   "Thank you all. [Name] will facilitate next week."
    *   End on time. Every time.

#### Steward playbook
> **Your job in one sentence:** Keep the money transparent and clean.

*   Maintain one shared ledger. Update it within 24 hours of any transaction.
*   Every member has read access at all times. No exceptions.
*   Report on the ledger at any gathering where resources are relevant.
*   Never spend shared resources without full Circle consent.
*   Never receive personal financial benefit from Circle assets.
*   If in doubt about any transaction, bring it to the full Circle first.
*   At Season end, present a final ledger summary at the Retrospective.
*   Hand the ledger to your successor with full context documented.

#### Scribe playbook
> **Your job in one sentence:** Keep the record alive and accessible.

*   Take brief notes at each gathering: topics, decisions, commitments.
*   Publish notes to the group thread within 48 hours.
*   Maintain the Season record: Opening intentions, weekly logs, Retro summary.
*   The notes belong to the Circle. Make sure every member can access them.
*   At Season end, compile the Season Summary: learnings, patterns, one improvement.
*   Hand the record to your successor clearly organized and complete.

#### Bridge playbook
> **Your job in one sentence:** Carry information both ways, commit nothing unilaterally.

*   Attend Mesh coordination gatherings each Season.
*   Report back to your Circle at the next gathering after every Mesh interaction.
*   You have voice at Mesh level. You do not have a vote that binds your Circle.
*   Any Mesh proposal that affects your Circle comes back to the full Circle first.
*   Flag any cross-Circle opportunity or conflict to your Circle promptly.
*   Maintain your Circle's entry in the distributed Mesh registry.
*   If you cannot attend a Mesh gathering, arrange a proxy and brief them fully.

### A final note

This handbook is a starting point, not a final instruction set.

The best Circles will adapt every section in this document to fit their people, their context, and what they discover through practice. That is not a deviation from the framework — it is the framework working as designed.

If you find something that works better than what is written here, document it and share it. The framework improves through the lived experience of the people who use it, not through the pronouncements of any central authority.

The goal was never the framework. **The goal was the people.**
